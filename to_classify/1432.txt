 I N THE AFTERMATH of the first world war, the gold standard inspired nearly religious fervour from central bankers.
The authors calculate the impact of a gold standard, had it been in place in 2000-20.
The central bank’s policy rate would have become a function of gold supply—the amount of metal mined—and gold demand from investors and households.
It requires Herculean assumptions for the gold standard to beat today’s regime, judged by gauges such as the volatility of inflation.
